Background

It’s no surprise that the widespread adoption of flexible, no-penalty booking policies by online travel agencies–such as Kayak, AirBNB, Hotels.com– has contributed to the sharp increase of hotel cancellations. In fact, a study by D-Edge Hospitality Solutions — which analyzed the performance of 680 properties in Europe between 2014 and 2018 — found that the average cancellation rate grew from 32.9% in 2014 to 39.6% in 2018 (Hotel Management). Even worse, online booking agency Booking.com saw a cancellation rate at 50% in the same year. The estimated $2.1 billion cost for the industry underscores the relevance of modern data analytics in the hospitality field (Prostay).

Traditionally, hotels have relied on overbooking. Overbooking is based on the assumption that some customers who have booked rooms will not appear for check-in (no-shows), others will cancel or amend their bookings last minute, while others will prematurely end their stay due to illness, personal reasons, weather, or other causes. However, overbooking can be tricky to do correctly, especially as cancellation behavior has grown more complex and dynamic.

A Cost-Sensitive Analytical Frame

To overcome limitations of static overbooking rules, we turn to stacked modeling. A stacked modeling approach–able to combine the insights of individual modeling behavior– therefore presents opportunities for tailored marketing and cancellation policies based on the characteristics of each booking segment. In brief, an ensemble model is capable of capturing segment-specific risk profiles and combines well with a cost matrix to reflect the asymmetric financial consequences at stake.

Kappa Analysis

Our approach first centered on prioritizing Kappa, an attribute of models that helps models achieve outcomes that are better than chance alone. For instance, a model that simply predicts “no cancel” for every booking would achieve high accuracy, but kappa would penalize that kind of naïve performance by comparing observed agreement against what chance alone would produce.

Stacked Model

Overview of Stacked Model Approach

The stacked model belongs to the family of stacked generalization (ensemble techniques) where a super‑learner (here, a decision tree) learns to combine base‑learner predictions in a way that can outperform any single model, strengthened by incorporating a cost matrix.

In practice, each base model first generates a prediction vector on the test set, and then these predictions are coalesced into a single data frame along with the true response to create a new, smaller feature space for the second‑level model.

In addition to combining the predictions of the five base models, the stacked model incorporates a cost matrix into the second‑level decision tree.

In the hotel context, a missed cancellation (false negative) means the hotel turns away too many guests; a false positive means the hotel may apply unnecessary retention interventions to a guest who would have stayed anyway.

By assigning a penalty each misclassification, a cost matrix helps the decision tree optimize for a cost‑sensitive error tradeoff.

Conclusion

The stacked model approach did not improve upon the strongest individual model. While their success rates did come very close to each other…

  • Decision Tree success rate: 80%

  • Stacked model success rate: 80%

  • Decision Tree cost: $423,000

  • Stacked model cost: $196,100

In this case, the stacked model was more cost-efficient. Due to the cost matrix, it was able to hone in the higher cost item, the missed cancellations. By implementing this model, Hotel 414 is able to efficiently retain customers and more accurately predict cancellations.
